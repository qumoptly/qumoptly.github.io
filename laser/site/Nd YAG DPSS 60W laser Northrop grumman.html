Nd: YAG DPSS 60W Northrop grumman laser



60W DPSS YAG single mode YAG laser 2x73mm

This laser head consists of a Nd: YAG bar of diameter 2mm and 73mm long doped with 0.6% of neodymium.
The faces of the bar are anti-reflective treated for 808nm and are convex to compensate for the lens effect of the bar.
The pumping is done by 3 rows of 3 diodes or 9 laser diodes of 20W each at 808nm.
The power supply is 18VDC and 26A max.
I will operate this laser in continuous mode, then in Q-switch with an AOM, then in MOPA (Master Oscillator Power Amplifier) ​​with a 2nd head with a bar of 3mm x 65mm.
For a first test, I will use a HR mirror with a bend radius of -700mm and a plane exit mirror with 80% reflection.

Q-switch MOUNTING with AOM (acousto-optic modulator)

The key element of an AOM is a transparent crystal (or piece of quartz) through which light travels. A piezoelectric transducer attached to the crystal is used to create a sound wave with a frequency of the order of 80 MHz. The light can then undergo Bragg diffraction at the mobile periodic refractive index grating generated by the sound wave; therefore, AOMs are sometimes called Bragg cells.


An acousto-optic modulator (AOM) is a device that can be used to control the power, frequency, or spatial direction of a laser beam with an electrical control signal. It is based on the acousto-optical effect, that is to say the modification of the refractive index by the oscillating mechanical pressure of a sound wave. The AOM, called Q switch, then serves to block the laser resonator before the pulse is generated. In most cases, the zero-order (undiffracted) beam is used under laser-effect conditions, and the AOM is activated when the laser emission is to be prohibited.

Q-switch passive ASSEMBLY with saturable absorber.
A passive saturable absorber is an optical component with some optical loss, which is reduced at high optical intensities. This can occur, for example, in a medium with absorbent dopant ions, when a high optical intensity leads to a depletion of the ground state of these ions. Similar effects can occur in semiconductors, where the excitation of the valence band electrons in the conduction band reduces the absorption for photon energies just above the bandgap energy.
    

Here are different types of materials and 2D structures that can be used for Q-switching or locking mode.
Saturable dyes and saturable glasses like Schott 695 were the first used since 1964.
SESAM mirrors came on the market around 1992 mostly used for mode-locking femtosecond lasers. At the beginning of the 2000s, 1D and 2D nano-structures appeared with a whole series of new bodies making it possible to reduce the duration of laser pulses.

MOUNTING Q-switch with a Pockels cell.

A Pockels cell is an element consisting of an electro-optical crystal through which light passes. Two electrodes are arranged on the crystal on which a voltage is applied which produces a phase retardation of the light. The Pockels cell behaves like a waveguide that can be modulated.

MOUNTING in "cavity dumping".
 MOUNTING in "cavity dumping". The installation in "cavity dumping" translated into French drained cavity! Is the opposite of the Q-switching where the laser energy is stored in the amplifying medium (population inversion) here the energy is stored in the laser cavity in the form of an electric field and when a voltage is applied to the cell from Pockel the stored energy is released thanks to the polarizer! The pulse duration of a laser using the "dumping cavity" is determined by the length of the resonator and the switching speed of the pockel cell.
